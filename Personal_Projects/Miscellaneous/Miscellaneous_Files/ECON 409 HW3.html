def heatmap(x, y, metric, values):
    
    # specify the columns I will be pulling from the results
    p2p = values[[x, y, metric]]
    
    # If p > 2, we need to group 
    heat = np.round(p2p.groupby([x,y]).max(),1)
    heat = heat.unstack()[metric]

    # round labels
    heat.index = np.round(heat.index,2)
    heat.columns = np.round(heat.columns,2)

    # make plot
    f, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(25, 12))
    ax = sns.heatmap(heat, fmt='.1g')
    ax.set_title("Heat map of "+ metric,size = 18)
    ax.tick_params(axis='both', which='major', labelsize=20)
    ax.set_xlabel(y, size = 18)
    ax.set_ylabel(x, size = 18)
    ax.collections[0].colorbar.set_label(metric, size = 18)
    sns.set(font_scale=1)
    plt.show()

def GINI_COEF(returns):
    # get the number of periods -> will allow us to calculate the areas
    periods = len(returns)
    
    # sort values and sum to calculate the lorenz curve
    LorenzCurve = np.cumsum(returns.sort_values(by = "Returns"))
    # start from 0
    LorenzCurve = pd.DataFrame({ 'Returns': [0]}).append(LorenzCurve)
    Line = LorenzCurve.copy()
    # Form the line that encompasses A and B
    Line["Returns"] = np.arange(0,1+1/periods, 1/periods)*max(LorenzCurve["Returns"])
    
    # calculate the area of A+B
    UpArea = 0
    for i in range(1,len(returns)):
        UpArea = UpArea + ((Line.iloc[i,:] - LorenzCurve.iloc[i,:] + Line.iloc[i-1,:] - LorenzCurve.iloc[i-1,:]) / 2)
    # calculate the area of A+B+C
    if min(LorenzCurve["Returns"]) < 0:
        AllArea = (np.abs(min(LorenzCurve["Returns"])) * periods) + ((max(LorenzCurve["Returns"]) * periods)/2)
    else:
        AllArea = ((max(LorenzCurve["Returns"]) * periods)/2)
    gini = UpArea / AllArea
    return(gini[0])

Question 4A¶

If investors believe that the Fed is merely posing in its stance on inflation, then they will continue to bet that the upcoming rate hikes are just wordplay to ease fears on inflation. Therefore, investors will continue to stoke the economy — thereby increasing inflationary pressures — and increase the challenge that the Fed faces in lowering inflation. Furthermore, this runs the risk of a rug-pull on investors who charge ahead: if the drop in rates does not occur as predicted, then this could decrease confidence in the economy and potentially spark a new round of instability; an unsteady economy generally entails some form of loss suffered by those with money in it.

Question 4B¶

Currently, the stock market is experiencing relative instability with little-to-no growth: the DOW Jones has seen a general adjustment of only approx. -1.47% over the last year (approx. -1.00% YTD).

I believe the primary reason for increasing stock prices despite all rationality is simple: instability brings with it a fantastic opportunity to make money, as long as you aren't the one holding the bag at the end. Another substantial secondary reason can be found behind the enormous quantity of money released into the economy throughout COVID. I believe that investors see the government rushing to prevent a total economic collapse during the early pandemic, then extrapolate it to any future crisis in the market. Of course, this isn't incorrect if the next crisis is as big and bold as COVID. However, such events are hallmarks of a generation and rarely (and hopefully) not repeated at such large scales.
